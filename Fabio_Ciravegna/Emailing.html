Sending a Message

Please Read

I value your message and I will do my best to answer promptly. And honestly I am a reasonably nice guy and try to be kind to others. However, we are all very busy and overwhelmed by far more messages than we can cope with. Here are some tips on how to write messages that attract my attention or at least get a reasonably rapid answer.

General tips

  • Write short messages. If your message is two page long, I am rather unlikely to be able to process and take it all rapidly;

  • Be very clear, by stating the topic of the message and any desired action in the first couple of lines. If I do not get what you want, I am very likely to set aside your message for another time.

If you must agree date/time for a meeting

  • Explain clearly what it is for. If you are a student, my office hours are on Thursdays afternoon

If you are submitting for an open position/studentship

  • Read the text of the ad carefully. Failing to do so is rarely the best of the introductions.

  • If the position has some restrictions (e.g. it is reserved to UK citizens) and you unfortunately do not meet them, please do not apply or ask to be considered. if there is a restriction it is for a good reason and I cannot change it;

  • Normally positions come with requirements, generally divided into Essential Requirements and Desired Requirements. Essential requirements are - as the word says - essential. If a PhD is required for a position and you do not have it, I cannot select you. Period. Please read the requirements carefully and ask yourself honestly if you meet them. There is no point in wasting your time. If there is a requirement, there is a reason. If there is a reason there is no way out.

  • Provide all the required information, including a vitae AND an accompanying letter: this is a letter explaining why you think you are appropriate candidate for the position. Some people are confused about the term accompanying letter. Let me explain. Your CV is generic. It states who you are and what you have achieved so far. It will say that you have studied at a specific university, you have obtained a specific degree, maybe worked here and there and so on. The CV will not change if you apply for different positions at different universities. The accompanying letter is a guide to your cv specifically written for the position you are applying for. It takes the requirements stated by the ad (see above) and shows in what way you meet them. For example if the position requires you to have written scientific papers, you should summarise your track record by saying something like "I have published X papers at international conferences which have Y citations, etc. I am personally particularly fond of letters which list each requirement as a subsection header and then explain for each of them how the candidate meets the specific requirement. This is not only useful to the judging panel. It is also useful for the candidate to understand if they meet the requirements and how well (and hence if it is the case to apply or not). Of course the letter is also the place to honestly mention if you do not meet a specific essential requirement. Essential requirements are - as explained - essential. However you may think you are a special case. For example if a PhD is required and you do not have it but can show that you have e.g. relevant equivalent research experience. The letter is your chance to show what the equivalent experience is. It is your best hope that the panel will be able to consider you (please however consider: exceptions are very rarely allowed!).
    Omitting information will get you nowhere.

If you are Italian

  • Yes I am Italian and my Italian is reasonably fluent. But, please, write in English, if possible. If your message is better answered by someone else in my group I will be able to forward your message to the appropriate person. If your message is in Italian I will have to answer in person and this could imply some serious delays.

If you want me to review for a journal or conference without having agreed in advance

  • Please do not send your papers. I am already overwhelmed by reviews and I can not accept one-off unsolicited invitations

If you are tempted to ignore the advice above, e.g. by writing: "I know that you suggest not to... but..."

  • Escape the temptation.
